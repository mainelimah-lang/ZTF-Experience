💡 Imagine each action in your life as a room. When you step into the room, the action begins. Once it’s done, you walk out and close the door behind you. You’re no longer inside — you’re now looking back at something that is complete and no longer happening. The Past Simple works the same way. It’s used to talk about things that happened, ended, and are no longer connected to the present.

🚪 Past Simple is like a door you’ve closed . The event happened behind that door, and now you’re outside, simply remembering or talking about what happened inside.

🗣️ Pronunciation

The -ed ending has three different pronunciations that significantly affect meaning and clarity.

/ɪd/ Sound

Used after verbs ending in t or d as an extra syllable.

  • started - /ˈstɑːrtɪd/
  • ended - /ˈɛndɪd/
  • wanted - /ˈwɑːntɪd/
  • decided - /dɪˈsaɪdɪd/

/t/ Sound

Used after voiceless consonants (p, k, f, s, sh, ch):

  • walked - /wɑːkt/
  • watched - /wɑːtʃt/
  • helped - /hɛlpt/
  • liked - /laɪkt/

/d/ Sound

Used after voiced consonants and vowels (b, g, v, l, m, n, r, z):

  • loved - /lʌvd/
  • claimed - /kleɪmd/
  • played - /pleɪd/
  • lived - /lɪvd/

The Past Simple helps us describe:

✅ Completed Actions in the Past
Use the Past Simple to talk about actions that happened once and are now finished.
  • I visited my grandmother last weekend.
  • She called her mother last night.
  • The movie started at 8 PM.
  • We finished our homework before dinner.
  • They moved to Madrid one year ago.

✅ Series of Completed Actions
Use Past Simple to describe a sequence of actions that happened one after the other.
  • I woke up, brushed my teeth, and had breakfast.
  • He arrived at the airport, checked in, and boarded the plane.
  • She opened the door, looked outside, and smiled.
  • He opened the door, walked inside, and turned on the lights.
✅ Past Habits and Routines
Use Past Simple (or used to) to talk about things you did regularly in the past but don’t anymore.
  • When I was young, I played football every day.
  • My father always read the newspaper after breakfast.
  • We traveled to Japan every summer.
  • I used to walk to school every morning.

✅ Past States
Use Past Simple to describe feelings, conditions, or facts that were true in the past.
  • She was happy at the party.
  • The weather was beautiful yesterday.
  • I felt tired after the long journey.
  • The restaurant was crowded on Friday night.
  • I lived in Brazil for two years.

👉 Patterns

We have talked about when we use the Past Simple. Now let’s explore how we use it:

✅ Affirmative Sentences

  • Regular Verbs
    • 👉 General rule → add “ed” to the end of the verb
      • walk → walked
      • play → played
      • watch → watched

    • 👉 If the verb ends in:
      • “e” → Add only "d"

        live → lived

      • Consonant + y → Change "y" to "ied"

        try → tried

      • Short verbs ending in consonant-vowel-consonant → Double the final consonant and add “-ed”

        stop → stopped

  • Irregular Verbs

    Irregular verbs don’t follow any rules. They must be memorized.

⌛ Time Expressions

The Past Simple is often used with a time expression to show when the action happened. These expressions help give context and make the meaning more specific.

📆 Specific time references

  • yesterday
  • last (night, week, month, year, summer, etc.)
  • ago (two days ago, many years ago)
  • in (a specific year, month, or part of the day)
  • on (a specific day or date)
  • at (a specific time)
  • this morning / this afternoon / this evening

📖 Narrative or Storytelling Expressions

  • once upon a time
  • long ago / a long time ago
  • in those days
  • back then
  • the other day

🌀 Frequency Expressions

  • often, sometimes, always, never, usually
  • every day, every week, every month

⏳ Duration or Time Span

  • for (e.g., for two years, for a while)
  • from... to... (e.g., from 2010 to 2015)
  • during (e.g., during the holidays, during the war)

🔢 Sequential Connectors

  • first, then, next, after that, finally
  • before, after, when, while

However, using a time expression is not always necessary.
Sometimes the time is:
  • already known from the context
  • not important to the message
  • or implied in the conversation

Did you talk to Sarah? Yes, we talked.
She didn’t like the food.
He arrived, opened the door, and sat down.
