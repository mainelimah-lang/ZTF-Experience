üí° The Present Perfect Tense is used to describe an event that occurred at an unspecified time in the past whose consequences are still active, visible, or important now. It is not just about what was done ‚Äî it is about what is true now because of it.

The present perfect creates an explicit link between past and present. It shows a direct cause-and-effect relationship between a past event and the current situation.

For example, saying ‚ÄúI lost my keys‚Äù clearly places the event in the past and focuses on what happened. The conversation will naturally move on to when it happened or what was done next.

But saying ‚ÄúI have lost my keys‚Äù does more than report a past fact ‚Äî it signals that the result is still true now: the keys are still missing. This has immediate, tangible consequences in the present ‚Äî you can‚Äôt get into your house, start your car, or maybe you are late for work because of it.

When you say ‚ÄúI‚Äôve lost my keys,‚Äù people immediately understand that you do not have them right now. The sentence brings the current situation into focus. In contrast, ‚ÄúI lost my keys‚Äù does not carry this implication; it simply describes a past event.

The simple past gives you the event,

This ‚Äúshadow,‚Äù however, fades when the result is no longer relevant. The relevance has shifted. The present perfect is used when the past event still affects the present, while the simple past is used when it no longer has an active impact.

The breaking happened at an unknown time in the past.
